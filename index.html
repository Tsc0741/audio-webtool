The Learning Gap Between Music and Acoustics

Traditional education often treats music and acoustics as separate disciplines. Music students learn about harmony, scales, and intervals through subjective experience, while physics students approach sound through wave equations and frequency analysis. This artificial separation creates a learning gap that limits understanding in both fields.

AcoustiLearn bridges this gap by creating a parallel learning track where perception-based musical concepts directly connect to their physics-based acoustic foundations. Every tool is designed to reveal the scientific principles behind musical phenomena while making abstract acoustic concepts tangible through auditory experience.

The Purpose: Unified Sound Understanding

This project exists to demonstrate that music and acoustics are not separate subjects but complementary perspectives on the same phenomena. By providing tools that operate in both domains simultaneously, students can:

  • Experience how mathematical frequency ratios create musical intervals
  • Understand why certain chord progressions sound pleasing through harmonic analysis
  • Connect their personal auditory perception to universal acoustic principles
  • See how psychoacoustic research explains musical phenomena
  • Bridge the gap between subjective musical experience and objective measurement

Interactive Discovery Learning

Each tool encourages exploration and discovery rather than passive absorption. The harmonic synthesizer lets you build complex timbres while simultaneously showing their spectral content. The psychoacoustic tests quantify your personal auditory capabilities while explaining the perceptual basis of musical harmony. The chord analysis tool reveals the mathematical structure underlying musical beauty.

Educational Applications

These tools serve multiple educational contexts:

  • Music theory courses exploring the physics of harmony
  • Physics courses making wave mechanics tangible through music
  • Psychology courses investigating auditory perception
  • Audio engineering programs connecting technical skills to musical intuition
  • Interdisciplinary courses bridging science and arts
  • Professional development for musicians interested in acoustics
  • Research in psychoacoustics and music cognition

My Sound Journey

My relationship with sound started with music and gradually evolved into scientific curiosity. What began as piano and cello lessons eventually led me down a path where I'm now trying to simulate and control noise as a PhD student. It's been a winding journey that connects the dots between "this chord sounds beautiful" and "what happens when sound enters a foam - why does the sound die down in there?"

🎵 The Musical Side

Started with the classics
5-6 years of piano and cello lessons gave me my first real taste of how sound works. Nothing fancy, just learning to hear when things were in tune and getting a feel for harmony.

Got behind the microphone
Spent a couple years as a boom operator for student films. Suddenly I'm thinking about room acoustics and background noise instead of just playing notes.

Fell into live sound
4 years helping student performances with their sound systems. This is where it clicked - I could hear what sounded good, but now I needed to measure it, control it, make it happen reliably.

Became a live music junkie
4 years immersed in Taiwan's indie music scene, catching both local Taiwanese bands and visiting Japanese acts. Every show taught me something new about how different musical styles and venues approach sound.

Home studio tinkering
Started covering and mixing songs at home, trying to bridge what I knew from live sound with bedroom production.

🔬 The Academic Side

Started with speakers
Bachelor's work in loudspeaker production and testing. Learning how electrical signals actually become the sounds we hear - pretty mind-blowing when you first see it happen.

Moved to rooms
Master's in room acoustics research. Turns out the space around the speaker matters just as much as the speaker itself. Who knew?

Now tackling the big picture
Currently a 1st year PhD student in Mechanical Engineering at Purdue, focusing on noise simulation and control. I'm diving into the theoretical foundations of noise mitigation materials and developing simulations to predict how sound-absorbing materials actually work.

It's been a progression from "how do we make sound?" to "how do spaces change sound?" to "how do we control sound everywhere?"

Why This Matters

Here's the thing - I've been on both sides of this. I know what it feels like to intuitively understand that a major third sounds "happy" and also to calculate that it's a 5:4 frequency ratio. I've spent hours chasing the perfect mix in a home studio and also written code to simulate acoustic environments.

Most people get stuck in one world or the other. Musicians often think the science will kill the magic. Engineers sometimes miss how subjective and personal sound perception really is. AcoustiLearn exists because I've seen how much richer your understanding becomes when you can move between both perspectives.

These tools come from real experience - from adjusting EQ by ear in a live venue and then measuring the frequency response, from hearing how Taiwanese and Japanese bands create different atmospheres in the same Taiwan venues, from understanding both the emotional impact of a chord progression and the physics of why certain harmonics clash.
